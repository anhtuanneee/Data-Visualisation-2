Fuel in Australia · 2010-2025

Prices, Supply & State Differences

Every day, millions of Australians fill up their tanks. This visualisation explores how fuel prices have changed, where fuel is used, and how secure our supply really is.

Tran Anh Tuan Nguyen
FIT2179 Data Visualisation 2
May 2026

Chart 1

Petrol and diesel moved together, until diesel broke away.

Regular petrol, premium petrol and diesel mostly followed the same broad pattern. The clearest exception is 2022, when diesel rose above petrol during the global fuel price shock.

Chart 2

Road fuel demand follows different state rhythms.

These small multiples show annual petrol and diesel sales combined. Each panel uses its own scale so the shape of change is visible for large and small states.

Chart 3

Diesel carries more road fuel demand than petrol.

In 2025, diesel sales were higher than petrol sales in every state and territory shown. The pale bars show total road fuel demand, while the dots show the petrol-diesel split.

Chart 4

Diesel now takes the largest share of fuel sales.

Product sales show a wider fuel mix story: petrol's share has gradually fallen, diesel has grown, and aviation fuel dropped sharply during the pandemic before recovering.

Chart 5

Australia's fuel supply is increasingly import-heavy.

Combining imports with domestic crude oil and condensate production shows the supply side of the story. By 2025, imports made up more than four-fifths of this combined liquid fuel volume.

Chart 6

Diesel reliance is strongest outside the south-east.

The map shades each state and territory by diesel's share of road fuel sales, while proportional circles show total petrol and diesel demand. Western Australia and the Northern Territory are the most diesel-heavy, while NSW and Queensland carry the largest total volumes.

Chart 7

Australia's petrol tax is low by OECD standards.

The grey dot marks the price before tax, the gold dot marks the pump price, and the line between them shows tax added. Australia sits lower because its tax component is much smaller than in many European markets.
